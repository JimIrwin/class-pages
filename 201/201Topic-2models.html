PPF -- the production possibilities frontier

Production Possibilities

An economy's capacity to produce depends on two things: technology (---?---)  and resources (--? ? ? --).

Technology: knowledge about production (outputs, inputs, processes, institutions ... ). Technology (knowledge) is of primary importance because technology is required to have production (outputs and inputs and processes are all part of technology).

Resources (L K T), aka inputs or factors of production: labor (L), capital (K), and natural resources (T). Note that the basic concepts are enough for ECO 201; the detailed discussion that follows is not required material, but it might help you understand the basic concepts better.

L stands for labor, which is human time and effort at production (of any skill & pay -- whether by a dish-washer or a doctor, a movie star or an usher). Labor includes the skills and abilities which people get from education, training, and experience. Labor is often described as "human capital" because "capital" refers to produced resources. 

K stands for capital, which refers to produced resources, such as buildings, machinery, equipment, tools, and raw materials. Capital means "produced resources" and the production of capital is called "investment." Note that in finance and in popular usage, "investment" often refers to buying shares in a company or putting money in a savings account; but those are not "investment" in the economic meaning of the term (those are purchases of assets).  Economists describe education as investment in "human capital" because education increases the quantity and quality of work a person can do. And economists refer to labor as "human capital" because a person's ability to work is produced from education, training, and experience. In 201, unless otherwise stated, capital will refer to non-human produced resources. 

T stands for natural resources -- the naturally occurring resources (traditionally land, forests, minerals, oceans and other waters; now we also include weather and the environment more generally). Sometimes we will abstract from natural resources and just talk about human and nonhuman resources (maybe a good idea since natural resources need to be found and accessed to be used in production, so in a sense they end up being a produced resource (capital)).

We can get more output from either or both of more resources and more technology; the key distinction between the two is that technology is non-rival and resources are rival. Non-rival means that simultaneous use by any number is possible. For example, suppose you want to cook some fresh corn: boil water, add corn and simmer for 3 minutes. Any number of people can use that recipe at the same time; but the stove, pot, and water can only be used one at a time. The recipe (technology) is non-rival, the equipment (capital) is rival. 

[Real world extension: patent and copyright law is an effort to defeat non-rivalness. E.g. when one company owns a drug patent (or patent on X) then it has the legal right to prevent others from producing the drug (or whatever X is).]

Two key properties: 1) the PPF has a negative slope; 2) the PPF has an increasingly negative slope (left to right).

Possibly helpful hint: the slope is the cost of the X-axis good in terms of the Y-axis good given up (and small slope means the X-good has a small cost).

The negative slope of the PPF illustrates the economics concept of cost. The cost of something is the value of what is given up (to get that something).  Thus, if the model economy is producing 30 suits and 910 loaves, we would say that producing 10 more suits would cost 70 loaves, or an average cost of 7 loaves per suit (we could also say that producing the 30 suits cost 90 loaves, or an average cost of 3 loaves per suit). If we then went from 40 to 50 suits, it would cost us 90 suits (9 loaves per suit, on average).  Note that we can use the same logic to describe costs of food production -- starting at 40 suits and 840 loaves, producing 70 more loaves would cost us 10 suits, or an average cost of 1/7 suits per loaf.

When we move to models with more than 2 goods, we will measure costs in terms of money, but we will still be concerned with costs in terms of goods.Maybe we'll see a model with suits priced at $40 each, loaves priced at $2 each, and baseball hats priced at $10 each. If so, one suit is worth 20 loaves or 4 hats; one loaf is worth 1/20 of a suit or one 1/5 of a hat; one hat is worth 1/4 suit or 5 loaves.  In more practical terms, suppose you see a fancy shirt that would cost you $300; the economics perspective is to think about how much other stuff you could get for $300.

Inefficient mean wasteful (but wasteful of what?).  When production is inside the PPF then it is inefficient, because we are wasting the opportunity to have more production.  More formally, production is inefficient if it is possible to produce more of one good without producing less of another.  And production is inefficient if it is possible to the same amount with less input. The term "technically efficient" is used to describe production that is on the PPF (so more of one good cannot be produced except by producing less of some other good).  When production is on the PPF it could be efficient, but not necessarily -- that depends on how the values of goods. For example, in the PPF above, if suits are worth $40 each and loaves are worth $2 each then producing 10 suits and 990 loaves would be wasteful (because we could produce 10 more suits and 30 fewer loaves, getting $400 of clothing at a cost of just $60 of food). 


We will use the PPF in a number of ways.  Most simply, we move along the PPF to model changes in resource allocation (moving some resources from producing one good to producing the other).  We use production points inside the PPF to model unemployed resources such as in a recession.  And we model the effects of changes in resources or changes in technology by shifting the PPF -- this will be useful for exploring economic growth and other long-run macro issues.

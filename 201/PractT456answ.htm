1. Given the following information for a hypothetical economy:

year    production                         prices per
        shirts    bread    pizza            shirt    bread    pizza         y -- BY 1     y -- BY 2    y -- BY 3

1        20        400        100            $20        $2        $6        $1800         $2700      ($3800)
2        25        300        120            $30        $3        $9        $1820         $2730       $3700
3        30        200        200            $40        $5      $10     ($2200)         $3300       $4200
 

a. Compare the outputs of years  1 and 2.  

Year 2 output was 1.1% greater than year 1 output (1820/1800=1.0111 ); using year 2 as the base year gives the same answer (pure inflation).   
[comment: using year 3 as the base we see an index number problem, with year 2 output less than year 1 output (3700<3800)]

b. Compare the outputs of years  2 and 3. 

With year 2 as the base, year 3 output is about 21% greater than year 2 output (3300/2730=1.2088); with year 3 as the base, year 3 output is only about 14% greater (4200/3700=1.1351). The two different comparisons is an index number problem (pizza prices grew least and  bread prices grew most).

c.  Compare the average level of prices in year 2 and year 1.  Year 2 prices are 150% of year 1 prices (pure inflation). 

d. Compare the average level of prices in year 3 and year 2. 

With year 2 as the base, year 3 prices are about 36% above year 2 prices (3700/2730=1.355); with year 3 as the base, year 3 prices are only about 27% higher (4200/3300=1.273).  The two different comparisons are an index number, because of impure inflation.
 [Optional A-level comment: compared to year 2, year 3 production has more pizza and less bread , so it makes sense that inflation looks lower when year 3 is the base].

e. Suppose the share of output going to the rich (richest 1/5 of households) was 40% in year 1 and 50% in year 2. Compare year 2 to year 1 in terms of how much output most people got.  
  In year 1, the non-rich got 60% of output, or $1080 (year 1 $).  In year 2 the non-rich got 50% of output, or $910 (year 1 $). In this example, most people got less output in year 2 than in year 1, because their share of output declined a lot more than the economy grew.
 
 

2.  The following are data on the U.S. economy.

year            1950       1975      1995
GDP            295         1631      7270    billion current $
deflator        23.3       42.1        107.5  (b.y. 1992)
population   152        216          263    million people

 

a.  Calculate real GDP for  each year (what are the units ?)
 

we divide nominal GDP by the price index (%) to get real GDP  --

 

                     1950   1975   1995

real gdp:     1266    3874    6763    billion 1992 $

 

b. Calculate (real) per capita GDP for each year.
 divide by population to get per capita ...

                                 1950    1975     1995

per capita gdp:       8,329   17,935    25,714  1992 $

c. Rank the periods in terms of measured economic growth.

 

Compare pcGDPs:  17935/8329=2.15      25714/17935=1.43

 

So from 1950 to 1975 pcGDP grew by a factor of 2.15 and then from 1975 to 1995 it grew by a factor of 1.43.  The later is slower growth (but it may not be obvious,  since it is a  5-year shorter period).

 

To be precise, using LN's, we find that 1950-1975 growth was 76.7 % (=LN(17935/8329)),  an average of 3.07 % per year.  For 1975-1995 we find growth is 36.0 % (=LN(25714/17935) or an average of  1.80 % per year.  NOTE: The period 1950-75 was part of the post-war economic boom, perhaps the "best" period ever in terms of measured economic growth in the US.

 

d. The share of GDP going to the rich (top 20%) rose from 40% in 1975 to 50% in 1995. Based on that increased inequality, did most people in 1995 get less goods and services in 1995 than in 1975 (explain) ?
For each year, look at the product of the nonrich shares of GDP and the pcGDP :   60%  of  17,935 = 10761 in 1975,  50% of 25,714 = 12857 in 1995.   12857/10761=1.195  These data indicate that the nonrich (80%)  of 1995 had about 119.5% as much goods and services as did the nonrich of 1975. So the data indicate that most people in 1995 got more goods and services than in 1975.

The Gains from Trade Model

For each case of production information, try the following four questions (with the first two being the most important):

a.. Explain the pattern of comparative advantage.

b. Explain whether they can gain from trade.

c. Discuss:  is their trade unequal? is it fair?

d. Show that production is inefficient if it does not follow comparative advantage (e.g. if both agents do both activities)

            

 

             food             clothing

Jo     1 hr/loaf         7 hrs/suit

Ed     4 hrs/loaf     12hrs/suit
 

 

To answer we can look at costs of making suits:

 

Jo gives up 7 loaves by making a suit

Ed gives up 3 loaves by making a suit

 

And apply that info to answer the questions  ...

 

A.       A.  From the costs, we see Ed has the comparative advantage in suits (3<7), and therefore Jo has CA in bread.

 

B.   From the costs, we see how they can gain:   

Jo will gain is she pays less than 7 loaves for a suit

Ed will gain if he gets more than 3 loaves for a suit

And so They’ll both gain if Jo pays Ed between 3 and 7 loafs for 1 suit.

  

C.  The trade will be unequal  because Ed is working longer for the trade.   Ed takes 12 hours to make the suit and  Jo works less than 7 hours, because Jo makes less than 7 loaves.  Fairness is normative, so whether or not their trade is fair is a value-judgement.  Someone might argue that the trade is unfair because it is unequal. But my view is that if they voluntarily trade and they both gain  then it seems fair to me.

 

 

D.      If both Jo and Ed are making both suits and loaves then it is inefficient.  Without changing the time they each work, we could get more produced. We could get Jo to make one less suit and 7 more loaves (in the same time); and

we could get Ed to make one more suit and 3 fewer loaves (in the same time).  Then without changing the time worked we would get 4 more loaves and the same number of suits.  Since we could get more produced in the same time, production is inefficient. Every time that we have Ed make a suit instead of Jo making it we can get 4 more loaves made.  (And every time we have Jo make a loaf instead of Ed we can get 4/21 more suits).

Oops! this answer confuses unfair (normative) with unequal (positive).

 

The trade will be unfair  unequal  because Ed is working longer for the trade.   Ed takes 12 hours to make the suit and  Jo works less than 7 hours (to make less than 7 loaves).  Unequal trade is a positive concept, it means one side spends more time on the trade.   Some people might argue that the trade is also unfair because it is unequal (positive), but that is a value-judgement, and a value-judgement is not true or false.  Unfairness is a normative concept and is not true or false.

                   Orientation -- What is Economics About ?

Malthus, 1798: "I do not mean to enter into a philosophical discussion of what constitutes the proper happiness of man, but shall merely consider two universally acknowledged ingredients, health, and the command of the necessaries and conveniences of life." (Malthus, Essay on Population (1798), p. 109).

Marshall (1920, 1890) "Political Economy or Economics is a study of mankind in the ordinary business of life; it examines that part of individual and social action which is most closely connected with the attainment and with the use of the material requisites of wellbeing. Alfred Marshall, Principles of Economics, Book 1, Chapter 1, Sections 1 (8th ed. 1920, first ed. 1890). Also available at Google Books.

American Economics Association (AEA) 2012: "In short, economics includes the study of labor, land, and investments, of money, income, and production, and of taxes and government expenditures. Economists seek to measure well-being, to learn how well-being may increase over time, and to evaluate the well-being of the rich and the poor. The most famous book in economics is the Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of The Wealth of Nations written by Adam Smith, and published in 1776 in Scotland.    ... Although the behavior of individuals is important, economics also addresses the collective behavior of businesses and industries, governments and countries, and the globe as a whole."    from "What is Economics?" at the American Economic Association website.

A micro-oriented definition:  Economics is the social sciences that studies agents' choices and the coordination of agents' choices (based on lecture notes from Alan Stockman, 1999).

Another short definition: Economics studies the allocation of scarce resources in relation to human welfare (source: a variation on Robbin's 1932). 

Really short: "Economics is the study of how people choose to use resources."   (AEA 2012)

For an enriched approach to seeing what economics is about: "100 Years of the American Economic Review: The Top 20 Articles" AER (Feb. 2011). 

The AEA's video, intended to "to dispel entrenched misconceptions about who economists are and what they do. Economics can be broadly defined as the study of human behaviors aimed at finding solutions to help improve peoples' lives. Viewers are reminded that a degree in economics doesn't have to be about finance, banking, business, or government, . . . it can be useful to all individuals and can lead to many interesting and fulfilling career choices." 

Old assessment question for orientation and class discussion:   Which is the most fundamental to the study of economics?  
a. the stock market    b. inflation       c. unemployment       d. money     e. scarcity          

(not a typical test question, because we won't see much multiple choice) 

Correct answer is e.   Scarcity : something is scarce if and only if people want more than is freely available.  Economics focuses on scarcity as the primary obstacle to human happiness (welfare).  Don't confuse scarcity with poverty (but think about their relationship: we would not have poverty without scarcity, but we would still have scarcity if poverty was eliminated).

Inflation and unemployment and money are major topics covered in macroeconomics (and introduced in 201). The stock market is in the news everyday but it is rarely a big topic in economics; the stock market is studied in Finance, much of which is a field of economics.

Microeconomics (ECO 202/203) looks at individual choices, usually in the context of a market and supply and demand.  

Macroeconomics (ECO 201/204) looks at the economy as a whole, focussing on long term economic performance (economic growth) and short-run recessions and unemployment. Modern macro builds up from microeconomic principles. 

 

                        Foundation Concepts

Macro vs Micro

Short-run Macro -- performance of the economy from month to month, quarter to quarter, or year to year.  A central concern of short-run macro is  "Business Cycles:" recurrent fluctuations of GDP-growth and the unemployment rate (roughly opposite each other). Key SR Macro indicators: Unemployment  GDP-growth;

Long-run Macro : economic growth and welfare (pcGDP log-scale  decade to decade, and longer term), often in international perspective (e.g. interactive graph Extreme Poverty  Millenium Development Goals;   summary progress "report";   2012 Report).  

Circular Flow Model (CFM). A simple model of a market based economy, the CFM has two sets of agents who trade in two sets of markets, resulting in two flow, the real and nominal flows (the nominal flow is aka the money flow).  What key features of the real world are missing from the CFM?  Identify and describe one such feature; how would you modify the CFM to include that feature?  WARNING: one of the the textbook's "circular flow diagram" (section 1.3, p 14) is mistaken and misleading.

Below is a sketch of our CFM. 

 
The real flow of economic activity has households providing resources to firms, which use the resources to produce goods, which are then consumed by households. The nominal (or money) flow of economic activity has households paying firms to buy the goods in output markets; that money is the revenue of firms, which they pay to the households in input markets, paying for the use of the resources for production; in that part of the flow, the money is the income of households (which they use to buy the goods ... and so on).

The CFM is a very simple representation of a market-based economy, which abstracts from many important features of our economy (such as?).  Later in the term we are likely to refer to the CFM for some short-run macro analysis, when thinking about the role of money in the economy.

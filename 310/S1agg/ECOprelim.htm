-- orientation to economics, using an old principles test question:

Which is the most fundamental (or relevant) to the study of economics?  a. inflation;  b. unemployment;  c. money; d. scarcity;  e. the stock market.

 

Answer, scarcity.  Something is scarce if people want more than is freely available.  Economics focuses on scarcity as the main obstacle to human welfare (well-being).

 

 

"I do not mean to enter into a philosophical discussion of  what constitutes the proper happiness of man, but shall merely consider two universally acknowledged ingredients, health, and the command of the necessaries and conveniences of life."    (Malthus, Chap. 16; http://socserv2.socsci.mcmaster.ca/~econ/ugcm/3ll3/malthus/popu.txt)

 

Welfare :  well-being, or happiness.  In economics we focus on people's material well-being, as affected by their access to the goods that the economy produces. Economists recognize that people's welfare is affected by many other factors beyond the goods they get (for example relationships and health), but they abstract from psychological and spiritual and artistic issues and focus on materialistic factors affecting welfare.

 

Economics is the social science that studies the goal of welfare in relation to the problem of scarcity.  

 

Macroeconomics (ECO 201/204) is about the economy as a whole, e.g. long run economic growth and short-run recessions and unemployment.

Microeconomics (ECO 202/203) looks at individual choices; in our principles course, we usually look at choices in the context of a market.

 

ECO 310 starts with a macro perspective, with Aggregate Economic History.  When we switch to a historical perspective we will use more microeconomics.

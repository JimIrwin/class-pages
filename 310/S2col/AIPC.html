- what extent “exogneous” or “endogenous”?

Exog: diseases simply spreading and people dying (exogenous, one Euros arrive); “virgin soil epidemic” (lack of immunities to Old World Diseases). Wells/Welles “War of the Worlds”

Endog: historically contingent – factors spreading disease and preventing populations recovering …

1) power/war: warfare spreads diseases (armies carry them & refugees spread them); warfare disrupts AI economies, leaving AI more vulnerable to disease.

2) wealth/slavery (and other forms of forced labor) – AI enslaved and forced to work gold and silver mines. Bringing together people and exposing them to disease – horrible work conditions, underfed, overworked, unsafe work … AI more vulnerable to disease. Survivors take diseases back to home societies ...

3) religion/missions - similar effects from Christians trying to convert and protect (some nice, some imperialistic ...) -- mass baptisms and crowded sanctuaries spread disease. Offered shelter from slavers in crowded sanctuaries ... spread disease ....

Interesting article related to AIPC:  American Indian Population Recovery Following Smallpox Epidemics. A student explored this article (in 2016). A key implication from this article:  based only on the direct effects of epidemic disease, Amerindian populations would have fallen after contact with Old World diseases, but the populations would have recovered in some 50 to 100 years after the epidemics. Factors other than disease are required to explain the failure of population to recover (maybe social disorganization related to warfare and slavery?).  The article probably fits more easily with the view that the AIPC was not an inevitable result of contact (contrary to the Wells/Welles depiction of disease as deterministic).   Note that to make an argument that the AIPC was exogenous to European choices (at least after contact), one might emphasize the indirect effects of the medley of diseases (with social disorganization suppressing fertility and increasing mortality).

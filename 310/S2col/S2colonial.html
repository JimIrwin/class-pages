2018 note on slave trade mortality.  In their 2001 journal article, Klein, Engerman, Haines, and Shlomowitz report an 11.9% mortality rate in the "Middle Passage" (the transatlantic voyages) of the Atlantic Slave Trade (see Table V; estimated from the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database, based on 4,612 voyages). Two centuries earlier, England's anti-slavery campaigner "William Wilberforce had estimated that about 12.5 percent of slaves transported died in the Middle Passage."  And based on estimates by Thomas Cooper (another English anti-slavery activist) in 1788,  "the 10 million slaves arrived in the New World ... [at] a cost of 180 million lives" (p. 97;  over 90% of those lives lost in Africa, from warfare, violence, and disease associated with capture of slaves, transportation to the coast, and detention there).

2017 class-notes on the colonization of New England:   ~ 100 Pilgrims (1620)  and ~ 20,000 Puritans (1630s).    From an article in Newsweek,What's the Difference Between a Pilgrim and a Puritan? Pilgrims were Puritans who had abandoned local parishes and formed small congregations of their own because the Church of England was not holy enough to meet their standards. They were labeled Separatists. ...  Around a hundred Separatists left England in 1607-08 in search of religious freedom in the Netherlands; many of them later migrated to America in 1620 [founding the Plymouth Colony].   ... The far larger group, those we know as Puritans or Nonseparating Episcopalians, reluctantly retained attachment to the English Church ... These Puritans remained at home during the 1620s ...  In 1630, John Winthrop led some 1,000 English Puritans in the initial wave of the Great Migration to the Massachusetts Bay Colony, north of Plymouth.  ...  Fear of further repression quickened decision-making and, by 1640, New England colonies would be home to nearly 20,000 mostly Puritan immigrants."   In short: about 100 Pilgrims on the Mayflower  founding  Plymouth colony in 1620;  about 20,000 Puritans settling New England in  the 1630s.  Refer to the  article for additional perspective and insights.

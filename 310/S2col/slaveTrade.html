Lecture Notes on the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade1

(for wide-ranging information, see the website Voyages (including the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database)


Transatlantic Migration was mostly of African slaves for the first 3 centuries of European expansion into the New World. Only after 1840 do Europeans exceed Africans in terms of numbers of migrants to the Western Hemisphere (Eltis, 1983). By the second 1/2 of the 19th C the numbers of migrants from Asia is also huge, the second wave of "contract labor" or indentured servitude.


African migrants were taken in the transatlantic slave trade (1500-1880;

1 Slaves were people owned by other people, bought and sold as if they were livestock. It is now almost an extinct institution, but it was once very very common in most of world.2 Some scholars estimate that most people under the Roman Empire were slaves. New World slavery continued until the 1880's (Brazil). The trade into the US ended in 1808, and slavery in the US was abolished 1865. The British slave trade ended in the 1820's, with abolition of slavery in British colonies (e.g. Antigua, Barbados, Jamaica) in the 1830's.

2 Slaves were imported into the New World mostly to produce SUGAR -- the crop that was perhaps the oil of the time. Sugar was produced on very large plantations (100-500 slaves), mostly on Caribbean Islands and in parts of Brazil. Free people refused to work the plantations, but slaves were forced to. Probably 2/3 of slaves were brought over for sugar production. Most of the rest were imported for mining (gold, silver, and diamonds), but also for indigo, tobacco, coffee, and rice. COTTON was not important until much later (and mostly after US banned the trade (1808)).

3 The slave trade was one side of the "triangular trade" of the mercantilist nation states. The triangle had (1) European slave traders buying slaves on the West coast of Africa, paying with manufactured products (guns, knives, tools, clothing, liquor, sugar); (2) they took the slaves to the New World, and sold them to sugar planters and others; who paid with their receipts from (3) selling sugar and other slave-produced crops or minerals in Europe. Traders and trading companies were mostly organized and militarily protected by the Portuguese, English, French, and Dutch nation states; American colonists in New York, Philadelphia, and Boston also participated in the trade (until 1807). Europeans traded for slaves with kingdoms on the west coast of Africa. Coastal kingdoms engaged in warfare and raiding to capture and enslave people of inland societies.3 The newly enslaved endured a forced marched to the coast to be sold to the European traders. The impact of enslavement and transport to the coast was horrific, with at least 40 million people killed to "produce" some 13 million people for export (Klein, p. 34 in Drescher & Engerman 1998).

4. About 2/3 of the slaves in the transatlantic trade were men; there were very few children (children sold for less but took up almost as much room as adults, and they tended to die more).

5. Over 12 million slaves landed in the Americas in the period 1500-1880. A similar number were taken North through the Sahara desert from Africa in a longer span of time (about 1400-1900). Many more left Africa ... at least 1/10 of those leaving died in the "middle passage" of enslavement, between Africa and America.  Probably some 14 million left Africa bound for the New World. Many many more had been enslaved but died before leaving Africa (probably in the range of 30 to 50 million; but such deaths are difficult to distinguish for mortality associated with slave-raiding and warfare ). 

6 SIGNIFICANCE ?

- the evil that people have done to others.

- destruction of African societies and economies, roots of current African poverty

- role in financing European economic growth (the "Williams' thesis," one part of Eric Williams' (1944) classic Capitalism and Slavery)

- role in settling the New World, and providing markets that enable the 13 colonies to thrive.

- role in creating lower income societies in the New World outside of North America.

- real world face of MERCANTILISM

3 Eltis (1993, p. 1400) recently asked: " the question is not why slavery per se but rather which groups are considered eligible for enslavement ... what separates outsiders-those who are eligible for enslavement-from insiders, who are not. Thus Nathan Huggins has answered the often-asked question of how Africans could enslave other Africans and sell them into the slave trade with the astute response that the enslavers did not see themselves or their victims as Africans." In short, Africa included a wide range of societies, with people who did not identify themselves as African as opposed to European. Arguably, the development of European identity helps explain why people living in Europe were not enslaved to work in the mercantilist empires. On this point, see Eltis (1993) and more generally Patterson's Slavery and Social Death (1982), on the slave as outsider or "other".

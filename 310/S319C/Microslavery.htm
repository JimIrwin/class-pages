Microeconomics of slavery: Profitability and Productivity

Profitability?

For many years historians discussed whether a planter buying slaves made as much money as he could have by buying alternative "investments" (such as government bonds). Most famously, the historian Ulrich B. Philips pointed to rising slave prices and falling cotton prices in the decades before the Civil War to argue that southern slaveowners were caught in a profit squeeze. His explanation was that benevolent masters were sacrificing their wealth to keep the slaves on the plantations and protect the slaves and southern society from race war and genocide.  We now understand that both the rise of slave prices and the decline of cotton prices were a result of increasing productivity of slaves as the cotton kingdom expanded westward.

Profitability is no longer discussed much: economists have clarified that slave prices were determined in the markets of the South, so the profitability question is simply whether slave prices reflected slaves' earnings (like any other "capital" asset), or whether other factors like prestige affected slave prices.  Economists have found lots of evidence that slave prices were determined by slave productivity and no indications that prestige-seekers affected slave price.  The question now is why slave prices were so high on the eve of the Civil War. Perhaps slaveowners were extremely confident that slavery would not be ending any time soon, but maybe there was some sort of bubble in late antebellum slave markets.

Celine Clark provides some more info on Phillips; Fogel and Engerman, (Time on the Cross) discuss Phillips's idea of the "profit squeeze,"  and the profitability issue more generally.

 

The Productivity of Slavery

After Engerman (1967) discovered that the South in 1860 was not as poor as people had thought, he and Fogel started exploring the comparative productivity of agriculture in the North and South on the eve of the Civil War. First they drew on the 1860 censuses to measure agricultural outputs and inputs in the North and South and compare TFPs (total factor productivities).  Surprisingly, they found that Southern agriculture produced 40% more output from a given amount of labor, capital and land (the TFP of southern agriculture was 140% of the TFP of northern agriculture).  Critics argued that they were simply measuring the effects of the South's climate advantage, which enabled production of cotton (the most lucrative crop of 19th C US agriculture).  Fogel and Engerman (F&E) then drew on the Parker-Gallman sample of southern farms (from the US census manuscripts) to measure outputs and inputs of different sizes of farms/plantations within the South in 1860.

There has been a huge volume of work on this topic, but we can summarize F&E's results in the following table:

Comparative TFP by Slaveholding Size, Southern Farms/Plantations in 1860

       # of slaves                 0         1-15         16-50         51+

         TFP                     100         108           145         135

source: F&E, AER, 1977.

The TFP results are saying that plantations with 16 to 50 slaves were the most productive operations: the number 145 is saying that a given amount of labor, capital, and land used on a plantation (16-50 slaves) would produce 145% as much output as it would have produced if used on a slaveless farm in the South. Scholars continue to argue about why the slave-labor farms were more productive than free-labor farms.  My early work (Irwin 1988, 1994) focussed on the two main explanations: the slave-labor hypothesis and the plantation-efficiency hypothesis. The slave-labor hypothesis (Wright (1978) and Ransom and Sutch (1977)) says that slaves were forced to work more hours than free people chose to work, so slave-labor farms had more output because they had more input.  The plantation-efficiency hypothesis (Fogel and Engerman, 1974 and later) says that plantations were more productive than smaller units (economies of scale), because slaves in gangs could be forced to work faster and harder than workers on smaller units, and because centralized living arrangements reduced the costs of food and child-care and freed up female labor for field work.  Either way, slavery is seen here as a system that boosted the South's per capita GDP, compared to what a free-labor economy would have produced.

My research on Virginia in 1850-1860 (EEH 1988), and on the South as a whole from 1860-80 (EEH 1994) presents evidence and test to make a case for the plantation-efficiency story and against the slave-labor hypothesis.

Not surprisingly -- in view of the TFP evidence -- southern per capita GDP declined after emancipation. The debate is over why and how.  The slave-labor hypothesis says that slaves had been forced to work more, and that when freed they reduced their labor supply to be like free worker. It says the key factor to explain the decline of southern per capita GDP from 1860 to 1880 was the number of people who were freed from slave-labor.  The plantation-efficiency hypothesis says that it was the slaves on plantations who were forced to work more productively than free people would. It says the key factor to explain the decline of southern per capita GDP from 1860 to 1880 was the number of people who were freed from plantation-slavery.  Irwin (1994 EEH) uses econometrics to compare two types of areas in the South: (1) areas with plantation slavery and (2) areas with non-plantation slavery.  It shows that production increased  from 1860 to 1880 in the areas with non-plantation slavery and decreased in the plantations areas. Those results are  support for the plantation-efficiency hypothesis and against the slave-labor hypothesis.

 

The Historians' Perspective: What was life like for the slaves ?  (or, welfare issues)

Preample: some analogies to slavery ....

Elkins: the plantation as concentration camp, a "total institution" that infantalized the slaves and created "Sambos;" an emphasis on slaves as victims.

Fogel and Engerman: slavery as a gilded cage; slaves material conditions were better than those of the urban working class.

Genovese: patriarchy, the plantation as feudal estate. The class struggle between master and slaves results in reciprocial rights and duties (slaves long on duties and short on rights). 

 

 

1. Material conditions of slave life

 

punchline: overall, slaves' living conditions sustained hard work, with a growing population and political stability.

- slaves worked hard and producing lots of output

- slaves were reproducing like white population.

- slaves did not topple the system until the Civil War (when Union troops were on the way, masses of slaves rejected slavery and escaped North).

 

1. economists (Fogel and Engerman and later) show that southern slaves produced a lot (debate on how/why), and eliminate discussions that view plantations as chaotic and violent prison with starving inmates.

 

2. historians (Genovese, Blassingham and later) show that slaves were people, not passive victims, and shift away from discussions of how masters "treated" the slaves.  They help us to understand that slaves carved out space and meaning despite the overwhelming power of the masters.

 

3. economists and historians remind us of the contrasts between North American slavery and the sugar-producing areas (West Indies, Brazil: demographic experiences, long-run poverty).

The current puzzle:  the "dreadful childhood" of American slaves (according to Steckel*) 

 

In 1974 American slavery became easier to understand. Fogel and Engerman's Time on the Cross showed that southern slaveowners had a hard-working and fecund labor force. Genovese's Roll, Jordan, Roll showed why the enslaved people worked hard and raised children: they made the most of a bad situation, carving out space of their own to  nurture family, faith, and hope.

In 1986 American slavery became more difficult to understand. In a series of powerful papers Richard Steckel argued that southern slaves had "a dreadful childhood," suffering infant and child mortality rates that were worse than those typical of urban slums in the third world in the 20th Century.

Working from data on heights of slave children from shipping records, Steckel inferred that slaves had very low birth-weights and correspondingly high infant mortality. Working from plantation records on birth and deaths, Steckel estimated childhood mortality rates for slaves that were about double those of free children of the time. In Steckel's description, slave infants and children were malnourished and subject to high mortality until they joined the labor force, when the material conditions of life improved dramatically. Once in the labor force, slaves were amply fed, and they largely recovered from their "dreadful childhood," growing up to become the strong and hard-working adults familiar in Time on the Cross.

Steckel's results and speculations are fascinating and important, with huge implications for our understanding of slavery. If nothing else, they serve to challenge two guiding themes in the past 4  decades of historical research and interpretation (e.g. Blassingame, Gutman, Harding, Stampp, others): (1) the vitality and strength of the black family under slavery, and (2) agency -- the slaves' success in carving out room of their own and limiting the masters' attempts to dominate and dictate every facet of slave life. Those two themes need reconsideration if in truth southern masters imposed, and southern slaves accepted, a regime featuring a childhood of deprivation, neglect, and death; and an adulthood of hard-work fueled by abundant rations.

Steckel's findings have tended to be accepted by economic historians. However, the empirical basis for his view of slave childhood is narrow enough that additional research promises to be valuable. Steckel's (1986; EEH) result on slave infant mortality is based on his analysis of the heights of slave children from manifests of ships engaged in the coastwise trade in the antebellum period. His results on childhood mortality are estimated from records of 11 large plantations. It remains to be seen if the mortality experiences recorded there were typical of the southern slave population. In any case, more evidence, and more types of evidence, are desirable, because of both the importance of Steckel's findings and the narrowness of his evidence.

(excerpts from from J.R. Irwin, "How 'dreadful' a childhood ? Inferences on the Material Conditions of Slave Life from Price Data," University of Michigan Economic History Workshop, April 1998).
